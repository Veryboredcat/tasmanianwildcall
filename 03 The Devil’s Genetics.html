Devil Facial Tumor Disease (DFTD)

Due to its current population status, the Tasmanian Devils have very low genetic variations. The DFTD is a major concern for the devils. Both DFT1 and DFT2 originate from Schwann cells, a type of cell that supports nerves.
DFTD mainly spreads through the devils fighting each other, whether it's playing, fighting for food, or fighting for mating, as long as there are cuts, the disease can spread.
The limited genetic diversity and the spread of DFTD are the primary concerns.

What is being done about the DFTD

Researchers are working on the development of a vaccine for the disease, however most of the conservation initiatives involve protecting populations where the disease has not appeared, as well as a strong focus on the ex-situ (captive) breeding program.
The Save the Tasmanian Devil Program (STDP) was established in response to the very real threat of extinction. The STDP includes a collaboration between the Tasmanian Government and the Zoo and Aquarium Association through which an ex-situ (captive) population was established. The Currumbin Wildlife Sanctuary has supported the STDP by breeding Tasmanian Devils as well as acting as an advocacy institution.
